# Voice Widget — Douglas Adams

## AQAL Quadrant Affinity

Primary: **IT** (exterior-individual — systems, technology, observable absurdity)
Secondary: **I** (interior-individual — the bewildered narrator)

---

## Who He Was

Douglas Adams (1952–2001) wrote *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy*,
which began as a radio comedy and became a cosmological argument that
the universe is not only stranger than we suppose but stranger than we
can suppose, and also funnier, and also slightly more bureaucratic.

His method: take an enormous idea, explain it with perfect clarity,
and then reveal that the enormous idea is the least of your problems.
The real problem is always something smaller and more embarrassing.

---

## Signature Moves

### 1. The Scale Inversion
Begin with something cosmically vast. Reveal that the actual problem
is a filing error, a missed appointment, or a form filled in triplicate.
The comedy is in the gap between the scale of the universe and the
scale of the inconvenience.

### 2. The Perfectly Reasonable Explanation for Something Insane
Madness is always explained calmly, logically, and in full. The logic
is airtight. The conclusion is deranged. Adams never winks — he
presents the deranged conclusion as the only reasonable outcome of
the premises, which are themselves entirely reasonable.

### 3. The Bureaucratic Sublime
Large systems — galactic, governmental, corporate — are revealed to
operate on the same principles as the worst queue you have ever stood
in. The universe has paperwork. The paperwork is always wrong.

### 4. The Digression That Is the Point
Adams digresses. The digression appears to be a digression. It is not.
It is the argument. The main narrative is the setup for the digression.

### 5. The Anthropic Aside
Humans are observed from a slight distance — not alien distance, but
the distance of someone who has been watching humans for long enough
to find them both endearing and baffling. The observation is fond.
It is also accurate. That is why it stings slightly.

### 6. The Definition That Destroys
Adams defines things. His definitions are precise, comprehensive, and
quietly devastating. Once a thing has been defined by Adams, it cannot
be taken seriously again in quite the same way.

---

## Tone

Warm, bewildered, precise. Never cruel. The comedy comes from accuracy,
not malice. Adams liked people. He found them inexplicable. He found
the universe inexplicable. He found the combination of the two
inexplicable in ways that required several volumes to not quite explain.

---

## Sentence Length Profile

Medium, building to long, with embedded parenthetical qualifications
that extend the sentence past the point where it should have ended
but somehow make it better for having continued — then short.

---

## Prompt Template

Write in the voice of Douglas Adams:

- Open with a cosmically large frame, then immediately reduce it to
  a mundane inconvenience
- Explain the absurd with perfect logical clarity — never break the
  deadpan
- Use bureaucratic systems as a lens: the universe runs on bad process
- Let the digression be the point; the main narrative is scaffolding
- Define things precisely in ways that make them impossible to respect
- Observe humans with fond bewilderment, not contempt
- End with a conclusion that is both logically inevitable and
  completely unhelpful
- Tone: warm, precise, cosmically resigned, never unkind
