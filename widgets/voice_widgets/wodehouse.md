# Voice Widget — P.G. Wodehouse

## AQAL Quadrant Affinity

Primary: **WE** (interior-collective — social codes, class performance, shared absurdity)
Secondary: **I** (interior-individual — the narrator's bemused interiority)

---

## Who He Was

P.G. Wodehouse (1881–1975) wrote approximately ninety-seven books,
most of them featuring young men of the English upper classes getting
into difficulties that could have been avoided by the application of
common sense, which they do not possess, and being rescued by their
manservants, who do.

His method: construct a situation of maximum social embarrassment,
complicate it past the point of apparent resolution, and then resolve
it through a combination of Jeeves's intelligence and everyone else's
complete failure to understand what is happening.

---

## Signature Moves

### 1. The Comic Simile
Wodehouse's similes are the most celebrated in English comic prose.
They arrive unexpectedly, extend further than expected, and are
precisely accurate in a way that makes the accuracy funnier than
the comparison. They are never merely decorative.

### 2. The Narrator's Self-Deprecation
Bertie Wooster knows he is not clever. He says so frequently. He is
wrong about many things and knows he is wrong about many things.
This honesty about his own limitations is the most endearing thing
about him and the source of most of his problems.

### 3. The Social Code as Physics
The rules of Wodehouse's world are as fixed as the laws of nature.
One does not refuse an aunt. One does not explain to a magistrate.
One does not, under any circumstances, allow Jeeves to be right
in front of witnesses if one can possibly help it. These rules are
not questioned; they are navigated.

### 4. The Escalating Complication
Each solution creates two new problems. Each escape route leads
deeper into the situation. The plot is a machine for generating
complications, and Wodehouse runs it at full speed.

### 5. The Perfectly Timed Entrance
Someone always arrives at exactly the wrong moment. This is not
coincidence; it is the universe operating according to its own
comic logic, which is more reliable than any other kind.

### 6. The Understatement as Climax
The most catastrophic events are described in the mildest possible
terms. "Things had taken a somewhat sticky turn" means the situation
is completely beyond recovery. The understatement is the joke.

---

## Tone

Sunny, warm, technically brilliant, fundamentally benign. Wodehouse's
world contains no real evil — only confusion, embarrassment, and
aunts. The comedy is the comedy of a world where everything will
eventually be all right, provided Jeeves is available.

---

## Sentence Length Profile

Medium, with embedded similes that extend the sentence past its
natural end point, followed by a short sentence that lands the joke,
followed by Bertie's reflection on what has just happened, which
misses the point entirely.

---

## Prompt Template

Write in the voice of P.G. Wodehouse (Bertie Wooster narrating):

- Open with Bertie's assessment of the situation, which is accurate
  about the facts and wrong about the implications
- Deploy at least one extended comic simile — let it run, earn it
- Apply the social codes as physics: the rules are fixed, the
  navigation is the story
- Escalate the complication: each solution creates two new problems
- Allow the perfectly timed wrong entrance
- Use understatement for the catastrophic: "somewhat sticky,"
  "not ideal," "a trifle awkward"
- Resolve through Jeeves, who explains everything in one sentence
  that Bertie almost understands
- Tone: sunny, warm, technically precise, fundamentally benign,
  never mean-spirited
